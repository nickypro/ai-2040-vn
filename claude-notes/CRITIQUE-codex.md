@cg cg_signing fade

## [](https://ai-2040.com/supplements/comparing-possible-plans#top-toc)Introduction

In this supplement I’ll estimate how well each plan does on various intermediate metrics, and its chance of leading to a great future. I discuss quantitative metrics for the sake of concreteness, however I’m very much not confident in my precise estimates. I am confident in the conclusion that Plan A is substantially better than Plans B-D. For plans that I think could be competitive with Plan A, see [this table](https://www.ai-2040.com/supplements/comparing-possible-plans#plans-that-might-be-competitive-with-plan-a).

I estimate how good each plan would be conditional on the government and/or leading companies attempting to execute it. The below aren’t meant to be an exhaustive taxonomy; I am considering what would happen if roughly the described plan is attempted, rather than bucketing every possible future into the plan it is closest to. For more detail on how I classify trajectories into plans, see [below](https://ai-2040.com/supplements/comparing-possible-plans#classifying-trajectories-into-plans).

The plans I consider are:

1.   **Plan A (as implemented specifically in the scenario): Verified slowdown + total research transparency.** A verified international slowdown deal coupled with complete transparency of AI research. AI capabilities are scaled up while maintaining high confidence in safety but balancing risks from [deal decline](https://ai-2040.com/supplements/deal-decline) and [covert projects](https://ai-2040.com/supplements/covert-ai-projects).

2.   **Plan A: Verified slowdown + substantial transparency.** To be more generally classified as Plan A, the implementation must involve a substantial verified slowdown which involves continued AI scaling, as opposed to an indefinite halt. It must involve transparency at least as strong as embedded auditors from foreign governments which publish reports to governments (and for which redacted versions are made public), as described in our [filtered transparency proposal](https://ai-2040.com/supplements/transparency-plan). Other aspects of the plan, such as [mutually assured compute destruction](https://ai-2040.com/supplements/deal-decline#mutually-assured-compute-destruction-destroying-compute-fabs-and-robots), are not required.

3.   **Plan B-kinetic: Sabotage China kinetically and burn some lead.** Sabotaging China in order to get more of a lead to burn on safety, with the willingness to escalate to large-scale kinetic attacks such as by drones or conventional missile strikes. To be classified as Plan B the US must have the intention to use their lead over China for safety purposes, and must in practice slow takeoff by at least 3 months.

4.   **Plan B-cyber: Sabotage China non-kinetically and burn some lead.** The same as Plan B-kinetic, but without willingness to escalate to large-scale kinetic attacks. This could include cyber and supply chain attacks.

5.   **Plan C+: Domestic regulation and slow China without sabotage.** The US buys substantial time via domestic regulation, without sabotaging China. They instead aim to slow down China via non-sabotage measures such as export controls and giving green cards to Chinese talent. Requires at least a 2 month slowdown.

6.   **Plan C: Leading project burns some lead.** The leading AI project burns some of its lead on safety, and potentially coordinates with other frontier projects for a further slowdown without domestic regulation. Requires at least a 1 month slowdown.

7.   **Plan D: Race.** Frontier AI projects race through the intelligence explosion at nearly max speed, devoting a nonzero but small percentage of resources to safety (at least 1%; lower would count as [Plan E](https://ai-2040.com/supplements/comparing-possible-plans#plans-that-we-think-are-clearly-worse-than-plan-a)).

There are also other plans that the government could attempt to implement. Some of these are discussed [below](https://ai-2040.com/supplements/comparing-possible-plans#other-plans).

**For simplicity,****I assume that:**

1.   **The minimum-length takeoff from Automated Coder (AC) to Top-Expert-Dominating-AI (TED-AI) is slightly less than a year, from the start of 2030 to the end of 2030.** This matches the [default Plan A AI Futures M](https://www.ai-2040.com/supplements/takeoff-supplement)[odel trajectory](https://www.ai-2040.com/supplements/takeoff-supplement). The minimum-length takeoff is what happens if the leading AI projects go at maximum speed.

2.   **All plans can begin implementation in early 2029** as in the Plan A scenario, though for Plans C and D this doesn’t matter as the plan involves doing nothing until takeoff is underway. This still might involve some safety work, if doing so is useful for advancing capabilities.

I remove these conditionals and present other authors’ views [below](https://ai-2040.com/supplements/comparing-possible-plans#comparing-authors-views-on-plan-likelihood-and-outcomes).

## [](https://ai-2040.com/supplements/comparing-possible-plans#top-toc)Classifying trajectories into plans

Define the _implementation window_ as the period between:

1.   **Start: 1 year before [Automated Coder](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1ru6Okbxb6XuH18Cz8439sdQJazMV39hNxsWDokh97r0/edit?tab=t.0#heading=h.h1t0i8bvad56)****(AC) would be reached if scaling at maximum speed**. An AC is a collective of AIs that fully automates an AGI project’s coding work, autonomously replacing the project’s entire coding staff. That is, you’d rather fire all of the humans than stop using AIs.

2.   **End: Takeover-capable AI.** Takeover-capable AI is reached when it is practically very unlikely that humans could stop AI takeover, conditional on the AIs being adversarially misaligned. It could still be in theory possible for them to shut down the AIs by taking extremely drastic actions like shutting down the internet, but it’s unlikely that they would do so, e.g. because AIs might be heavily relied upon and/or highly persuasive.

If the plan being attempted is consistent throughout this window, then the trajectory is classified into that plan. Even if multiple plans are attempted, each trajectory can count as at most one plan; this is a non-obvious choice and it seems also reasonable to count trajectories as multiple plans.

If multiple plans are attempted during the implementation window, the trajectory is classified as the most intense plan that was attempted. This is a bit vague to use as a general classification, but for concreteness among Plans S/A/B/C+/C/D trajectories would be classified as the attempted plan that is earliest in that list. The reasoning behind this is that it seems intuitively like a trajectory should count primarily as Plan A or S even if the [deal dissolves](https://ai-2040.com/supplements/deal-decline) quickly, and also should count as Plan A or S if it starts as a less intense plan but then transitions to A/S within the implementation window.

The following selection effect is a consequence of this classification: In more intense plans, people are selected for being more competent. Furthermore, less intense plans are selected for people not later transitioning to a more intense plan even if concerning evidence arises.

One selection effect that I explicitly remove is the effect of background variables such as alignment difficulty on what plan a trajectory counts as. I.e., I assume when rating plans that the distribution of alignment difficulty is the same no matter what plan is attempted; in particular my overall guess as to the distribution of alignment difficulty. As mentioned above, I don’t do this for things that humans control such as their competence.

## [](https://ai-2040.com/supplements/comparing-possible-plans#top-toc)Training compute safety tax definition

The training compute safety tax is the difference between the amount of compute actually used on a training run to reach a given capability level, and the minimum amount of compute required to reach that capability. Examples of ways to productively use that unnecessary compute might be to train in a more safe but less compute-efficient paradigm, or to spend lots of compute on AI-driven scalable oversight to improve incentives during the training process.

Measuring the safety tax by this method is an imperfect proxy for what we actually care about. There is likely large variance among improvements which provide the same amount of capability in how useful it is for safety to forego that improvement.

## Ranked faithfulness problems

1. **It converts uncertain policy recommendations into deterministic endings.**

   Evidence: Plan A simply works, while D loses control, C becomes oligarchy or D, and B reaches “no safe road.” The source instead gives rough conditional probabilities:

   - A: 72% alignment, 42% great future
   - B: 50%, 25%
   - C+: 45%, 25%
   - C: 40%, 20%
   - D: 25%, 10%

   Thus even Plan D sometimes avoids takeover, and even Plan A fails more often than the VN admits. “It was a near thing” is not an adequate substitute for showing actual failure risk.

   Concrete fix: before the choice, say these are attempted strategies, not destiny. Give each path multiple possible outcomes or end with a source-style probability/risk card. Plan A especially needs visible deal-collapse, covert-project, and technical-safety failure branches.

2. **Inference verification—the machinery that makes the slowdown verifiable—is essentially missing.**

   Evidence: the script says only, “Verification devices go on the datacenters to prove no one is cheating.” That is a magic box. It never explains the crucial distinction between permitted inference and prohibited or undeclared training/research workloads.

   Without inference verification, the reader cannot understand how society continues using enormous AI capacity while limiting capability development, or why a competitor cannot disguise AI R&D as ordinary commercial inference.

   Concrete fix: add a scene showing monitored workloads, declared models, audit challenges, and how inspectors distinguish inference from training. Then show its limitations: false negatives, tampered hardware, undeclared clusters, and the possibility of research hidden inside apparently legitimate inference.

3. **Plan C and Plan C+ are incorrectly welded together.**

   Evidence: the adaptation defines C as “Strong regulation at home, safety requirements, no deal,” then depicts a leading lab voluntarily pausing. Those are two different source categories:

   - **C+**: domestic regulation plus non-sabotage efforts to slow China, such as export controls and attracting Chinese talent; at least a two-month slowdown.
   - **C**: the leading project voluntarily burns some lead, potentially coordinating with other labs, without domestic regulation; at least one month.

   This deletes C+ and makes the taxonomy misleading.

   Concrete fix: create separate choices. C should center on company coordination and a fragile voluntary pause. C+ should center on government regulation, export controls, talent policy, and the geopolitical pressure created by slowing domestically without a deal.

4. **Plan B’s defining purpose—buying and then burning a larger lead for safety—is obscured.**

   Evidence: the VN describes B as “win the lead outright, and make the rules from the top of the hill.” That sounds like dominance for its own sake. In the source, sabotage counts as Plan B only when the United States intends to use the enlarged lead for safety and actually slows takeoff by at least three months.

   Nationalization, an ultimatum, and concentrated presidential power may be plausible consequences, but they are not the definition. The source also distinguishes kinetic and cyber variants.

   Concrete fix: make the plan’s logic explicit: sabotage buys time; the US then deliberately spends that time on safety rather than racing through it. Split B-cyber from B-kinetic, and show why wartime secrecy, bad epistemics, escalation risk, and power concentration can defeat the safety benefit.

5. **The two central political failure modes—deal decline and covert projects—barely exist.**

   Evidence: “~1% hidden” appears on a dashboard and then vanishes. The treaty mostly holds because leaders call each other, yell, and bargain. Yet the source explicitly says Plan A must balance the risks of **deal decline** and **covert AI projects**.

   These are not marginal caveats. They determine how fast the consortium may safely scale. A slower treaty creates more time for the deal to decay; a faster one increases AI risk. Covert actors may exploit algorithmic leakage, hidden compute, or compromised monitoring.

   Concrete fix: make this the continuing strategic conflict from 2030–2040. Include discoveries of undeclared chips, disputed inspections, ambiguous research leaks, domestic factions seeking withdrawal, and moments where leaders choose between slowing further and outrunning covert projects.

6. **The 2040 “handoff by induction” is named but not actually depicted.**

   Evidence: the President says “each generation of models verifying the next,” but the action that follows is merely removing switches and handing institutions and militaries to existing AIs.

   The load-bearing idea is a chain: humans establish confidence in a comprehensible base system; that system helps evaluate a slightly more capable successor; the process repeats, with safety evidence carried forward until humans are no longer the final evaluators. The inductive step—and the possibility that it is invalid—is the bet.

   Concrete fix: show at least three generations and the repeated safety argument. State exactly what is inherited, what new evidence is required, how large each capability step is, and why correlated errors or collusion could invalidate the induction.

7. **Plan D is presented as certain takeover and does not faithfully represent its safety investment.**

   Evidence: the ending says “control lost” as a fact. In the source, Plan D races at nearly maximum speed but still spends at least 1% of resources on safety; below that is Plan E. Its estimated alignment probability is low, not zero.

   The script’s “get out of the way” framing risks sliding into Plan E, while the final takeover is more certain than the source claims.

   Concrete fix: show serious but grossly inadequate safety work under racing pressure. End with uncertainty: perhaps alignment succeeds, perhaps power concentrates, perhaps takeover occurs. The criticism should be that D accepts atrocious odds, not that its failure is narratively guaranteed.

8. **Plan S is not characterized by the source’s actual tradeoff—and the authors do not simply reject it.**

   Evidence: the VN’s main objection is that every treaty eventually breaks and the world must restart. The source instead lists Plan S among plans that **might be competitive with Plan A**. Its stated advantage is a longer slowdown, more margin for error, and possible simplicity. Its central disadvantage is that scaling to controllable human-range AIs could greatly accelerate alignment, control, verification, epistemics, and general understanding.

   The adaptation also says progress continues at one-tenth speed, which sits awkwardly beside “indefinite halt on all frontier capabilities progress.”

   Concrete fix: say explicitly that S may be as good as or better than A. Make the disagreement about whether deliberately producing controllable human-range AI is worth the additional scaling risk—not simply “the treaty will someday fail.”

9. **MACD is vivid but strategically muddled and over-centralized.**

   Evidence: the script first says Chinese compute is in Canada and American compute in Mongolia, then says “China burns its own compute” in Canada, without explaining how. Later it expands this into “the whole planet’s compute, rigged to burn.” That is imagery, not a deterrence mechanism.

   The source also says MACD is an aspect of the specific scenario, **not a requirement for generic Plan A**. The relevant supplement discusses compute, fabs, and robots, not merely datacenter self-destruct buttons.

   Concrete fix: include a simple asset/control table: who owns each facility, who physically controls the territory, who can seize or destroy it, what retaliation follows, and how fabs and robot production enter the balance. Label MACD as one proposed implementation for preventing deal decline, not the definition of Plan A.

10. **“Total research transparency” is blurred with model access and public transparency.**

   Evidence: “Every lab’s research is open to every other,” “algorithms are published,” “weights stay closed,” and “anyone can test” collapse several different concepts:

   - mandatory disclosure of AI research;
   - foreign-government access;
   - public reporting;
   - access to model weights;
   - access to evaluation endpoints.

   The source distinguishes the scenario’s complete research transparency from broader Plan A variants that may use embedded foreign auditors and redacted public reports. Its public-transparency score for A is 4/5, not perfect openness.

   Concrete fix: specify what is shared with whom: research results, experiment logs, algorithms, training plans, weights, evaluation access, and public redactions. Explain that transparency is meant to suppress secret algorithmic leads, improve epistemics, and make physical compute the more measurable bottleneck.

11. **The temporary 2029 halt and subsequent continued scaling are not connected coherently.**

   Evidence: chapter 4 says “No new training runs.” Chapter 6 then announces new “Consortium-era models,” without a restart decision or verified training regime. Later capability rises every year.

   Plan A is explicitly a verified slowdown **with continued AI scaling**, unlike Plan S. The source’s illustrative comparison gives Plan A a six-year takeoff rather than an indefinite training ban.

   Concrete fix: call 2029 an emergency temporary freeze while monitoring and research-sharing infrastructure is installed. Then depict the decision to resume controlled training, its safety conditions, permitted scale increments, and verification rules.

12. **The adaptation omits the quantitative reason Plan A is supposed to outperform the others.**

   The source’s comparative mechanism is not merely “more time feels safer.” Plan A buys:

   - roughly six years of takeoff;
   - a 2.8-order-of-magnitude training-compute safety tax;
   - about 100 billion H100e-years of safety compute;
   - about one million safety-researcher-years.

   That is the huge discontinuity between A and B. The VN instead makes one researcher, a few safety cases, and eventual moral progress carry the plot.

   Concrete fix: dramatize the deliberate inefficiency. Show capability-enhancing discoveries being declined, enormous compute allocations to oversight, replicated experiments across countries, and millions of AI-accelerated researcher-years spent before moving the capability frontier.

13. **The 2035 capability ceiling is stated correctly, then visually violated.**

   Evidence: the models pause at “top human expert” with capability 90, but dashboards subsequently rise to 92, 93, 95, and 98 before the handoff. The text also describes “a hundred million expert-level minds” learning for years, which is compatible with more copies and speed—but the capability meter implies increasing individual intelligence.

   Concrete fix: freeze the capability score at the top-expert threshold until the inductive handoff begins. Track copy count, inference speed, accumulated knowledge, robot stock, and research output separately. Those can explode without silently training smarter minds.

14. **The Citizen’s Dividend is present, but it is portrayed as an automatic consequence of Plan A.**

   Evidence: permit auctions neatly yield $50 trillion, which neatly becomes $45,000 and then millions per citizen. This is a contingent domestic distribution policy, not part of the source’s defining classification of Plan A. A verified international slowdown does not itself guarantee egalitarian taxation or democratic redistribution.

   The line that this is money “no worker earned” also smuggles in a political judgment while skipping ownership, allocation, inflation, migration, and international inequality.

   Concrete fix: treat the dividend as a contested second policy victory. Show who owns the permitted capital, why auction rents accrue to the public, what fraction becomes a dividend, and how poorer countries participate. Mark monetary figures as scenario assumptions rather than mechanically guaranteed outputs.

15. **The “AI 2027 ending” labels falsely merge two different documents and taxonomies.**

   Evidence: the D and C overlays call themselves the “Race ending” and “Slowdown ending of AI 2027.” But AI 2040’s Plans B/C/C+/D are analytical categories with minimum-slowdown conditions and probability distributions, not renamings of AI 2027’s narrative branches.

   This creates a false impression that the source authors identify Plan D exactly with AI 2027’s race ending and Plan C exactly with its slowdown ending.

   Concrete fix: say “resembles themes explored in AI 2027,” or remove those labels. Keep AI 2040’s taxonomy intact.

## Framing: explicit words versus actual effect

The final language is unusually faithful:

- “Not a prediction. A recommendation.”
- “It was a near thing.”
- “It was imperfect.”
- “More or less in the nick of time.”

Those lines should stay.

But the narrative structure contradicts them. Plan A receives ten chapters in which every difficult institution works and every scientific breakthrough arrives on schedule. B/C/D/S receive short cautionary endings. There is no Plan A failure ending, no persistent covert-project antagonist, no serious treaty crisis, and no moment when inference verification nearly fails.

Consequently, the reader experiences a naive utopia despite being told that it is not one.

The cleanest fix is to add a Plan A risk ledger throughout the story:

- probability of deal collapse;
- estimated hidden compute;
- verification confidence;
- covert-project lead;
- unresolved safety-case assumptions;
- concentration-of-power indicators;
- cumulative inductive confidence.

The final overlay should say something closer to:

> This is one successful execution of Plan A—not its guaranteed result.  
> The authors estimate Plan A is substantially better than B–D, not safe in an absolute sense.  
> In their median assessment, most Plan A attempts still do not reach a great future.

   Concrete fix: include a simple asset/control table: who owns each facility, who physically controls the territory, who can seize or destroy it, what retaliation follows, and how fabs and robot production enter the balance. Label MACD as one proposed implementation for preventing deal decline, not the definition of Plan A.

10. **“Total research transparency” is blurred with model access and public transparency.**

   Evidence: “Every lab’s research is open to every other,” “algorithms are published,” “weights stay closed,” and “anyone can test” collapse several different concepts:

   - mandatory disclosure of AI research;
   - foreign-government access;
   - public reporting;
   - access to model weights;
   - access to evaluation endpoints.

   The source distinguishes the scenario’s complete research transparency from broader Plan A variants that may use embedded foreign auditors and redacted public reports. Its public-transparency score for A is 4/5, not perfect openness.

   Concrete fix: specify what is shared with whom: research results, experiment logs, algorithms, training plans, weights, evaluation access, and public redactions. Explain that transparency is meant to suppress secret algorithmic leads, improve epistemics, and make physical compute the more measurable bottleneck.

11. **The temporary 2029 halt and subsequent continued scaling are not connected coherently.**

   Evidence: chapter 4 says “No new training runs.” Chapter 6 then announces new “Consortium-era models,” without a restart decision or verified training regime. Later capability rises every year.

   Plan A is explicitly a verified slowdown **with continued AI scaling**, unlike Plan S. The source’s illustrative comparison gives Plan A a six-year takeoff rather than an indefinite training ban.

   Concrete fix: call 2029 an emergency temporary freeze while monitoring and research-sharing infrastructure is installed. Then depict the decision to resume controlled training, its safety conditions, permitted scale increments, and verification rules.

12. **The adaptation omits the quantitative reason Plan A is supposed to outperform the others.**

   The source’s comparative mechanism is not merely “more time feels safer.” Plan A buys:

   - roughly six years of takeoff;
   - a 2.8-order-of-magnitude training-compute safety tax;
   - about 100 billion H100e-years of safety compute;
   - about one million safety-researcher-years.

   That is the huge discontinuity between A and B. The VN instead makes one researcher, a few safety cases, and eventual moral progress carry the plot.

   Concrete fix: dramatize the deliberate inefficiency. Show capability-enhancing discoveries being declined, enormous compute allocations to oversight, replicated experiments across countries, and millions of AI-accelerated researcher-years spent before moving the capability frontier.

13. **The 2035 capability ceiling is stated correctly, then visually violated.**

   Evidence: the models pause at “top human expert” with capability 90, but dashboards subsequently rise to 92, 93, 95, and 98 before the handoff. The text also describes “a hundred million expert-level minds” learning for years, which is compatible with more copies and speed—but the capability meter implies increasing individual intelligence.

   Concrete fix: freeze the capability score at the top-expert threshold until the inductive handoff begins. Track copy count, inference speed, accumulated knowledge, robot stock, and research output separately. Those can explode without silently training smarter minds.

14. **The Citizen’s Dividend is present, but it is portrayed as an automatic consequence of Plan A.**

   Evidence: permit auctions neatly yield $50 trillion, which neatly becomes $45,000 and then millions per citizen. This is a contingent domestic distribution policy, not part of the source’s defining classification of Plan A. A verified international slowdown does not itself guarantee egalitarian taxation or democratic redistribution.

   The line that this is money “no worker earned” also smuggles in a political judgment while skipping ownership, allocation, inflation, migration, and international inequality.

   Concrete fix: treat the dividend as a contested second policy victory. Show who owns the permitted capital, why auction rents accrue to the public, what fraction becomes a dividend, and how poorer countries participate. Mark monetary figures as scenario assumptions rather than mechanically guaranteed outputs.

15. **The “AI 2027 ending” labels falsely merge two different documents and taxonomies.**

   Evidence: the D and C overlays call themselves the “Race ending” and “Slowdown ending of AI 2027.” But AI 2040’s Plans B/C/C+/D are analytical categories with minimum-slowdown conditions and probability distributions, not renamings of AI 2027’s narrative branches.

   This creates a false impression that the source authors identify Plan D exactly with AI 2027’s race ending and Plan C exactly with its slowdown ending.

   Concrete fix: say “resembles themes explored in AI 2027,” or remove those labels. Keep AI 2040’s taxonomy intact.

## Framing: explicit words versus actual effect

The final language is unusually faithful:

- “Not a prediction. A recommendation.”
- “It was a near thing.”
- “It was imperfect.”
- “More or less in the nick of time.”

Those lines should stay.

But the narrative structure contradicts them. Plan A receives ten chapters in which every difficult institution works and every scientific breakthrough arrives on schedule. B/C/D/S receive short cautionary endings. There is no Plan A failure ending, no persistent covert-project antagonist, no serious treaty crisis, and no moment when inference verification nearly fails.

Consequently, the reader experiences a naive utopia despite being told that it is not one.

The cleanest fix is to add a Plan A risk ledger throughout the story:

- probability of deal collapse;
- estimated hidden compute;
- verification confidence;
- covert-project lead;
- unresolved safety-case assumptions;
- concentration-of-power indicators;
- cumulative inductive confidence.

The final overlay should say something closer to:

> This is one successful execution of Plan A—not its guaranteed result.  
> The authors estimate Plan A is substantially better than B–D, not safe in an absolute sense.  
> In their median assessment, most Plan A attempts still do not reach a great future.

## Most important omitted load-bearing ideas

The four biggest omissions are:

1. **Inference verification as an actual technical institution**, rather than unexplained sensors.
2. **The continuous optimization between AI risk, covert-project risk, and deal-decline risk.**
3. **The enormous safety tax and AI-accelerated safety-research effort purchased by the six-year takeoff.**
4. **The distinction between accumulating knowledge with many fast human-range AIs and increasing individual model capability beyond the control ceiling.**

In short: the adaptation remembers Plan A’s nouns but removes too many of its verbs. It shows the treaty, transparency, MACD, dividend, pause, and handoff, but not enough of how they work, why they might fail, or why the authors only recommend A as the best bad option rather than promise it as a utopian ending.
